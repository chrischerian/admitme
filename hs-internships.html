Securing Intenrships in High School

Bolster your resume with real world experience.


What is an Internship?

First of all, let us define what an internship actually is. Of the following, which qualifies as an internship, or to be more precise, something that colleges and universities will also view as an internship?

  1. Johnny attends a week long computer science camp run by Microsoft. He found and paid for this opportunity online. After arriving in Seattle, WA on Monday, he is paired with one of Microsoft’s programmers. During the week, he learns from this programmer in the morning. In the afternoon, he and the other members of the camp cohort work in pairs on their own independent project. Every evening, someone from the senior leadership gives a lecture on leadership, entrepreneurship, technology, and a variety of other topics. At the end of the week on Friday, Johnny gives a presentation about his work. He receives a certificate of participation for completing the program.

  2. Sally’s uncle is a cardio thoracic surgeon at Massachusetts General Hospital. He has agreed to let Sally shadow him for two weeks while he makes the rounds during normal work hours. She is allowed to observe while her uncle speaks to his patients, and she also uses this opportunity to speak to other doctors as well as some medical students about their respective jobs and reasons behind their career choice. Sally was even allowed to scrub in and observe one of her uncle’s surgeries. Based on this experience, Sally has decided to pursue the premed track in college.

  3. Ben spends an hour a week phone banking for a local campaign. The intern coordinator for a State Senate race sends him a list of phone numbers, and he calls them to talk about the importance of the campaign. Some of these phone numbers include high profile donors, which he takes detailed notes on. He forwards these notes to the intern coordinator, and sometimes his notes even get shown to the candidate.

All of these sound pretty shiny and great for the resume. Each on their own, all three are indeed good additions to any high school student’s extracurricular list. None are internships. All three demonstrate interest in a specific field of study or future career, but they are not good enough. Internships need some sort of what I like to call an end deliverable. An end deliverable is something to show for all the labor you put in during your internship. Did you get a paper published? Did you make a meaningful contribution to a project? What new skills did you learn, and in what meaningful way did you demonstrate your new skills? Anybody can receive a participation award. For an internship to be truly special, you need some tangible achievement.

An easy way to better develop a tangible achievement can be to look at your summer internship’s length. The three inadequate options given earlier help illustrate this tendency. If you are only able to work on a project for a week compared to someone else who works on the same project with the same intensity for an entire summer, the second person’s project will undoubtedly be more impressive. All three summer opportunities would become true, full-fledged internships if they were at least 1.5-2 months long, you worked for the full work day, and you used the time to create a solid end project.

Now, don’t balk at working at the same place for two months for your entire vacation. This is a small taste of what a full time career will look like. Use the summer to find out more exactly what you want to study in college and what industry you want to enter after graduation. If you don’t like the job, then that is also a valuable lesson learned. That job is not a good fit for you, and you should avoid it in the future. If you love the job, can’t wait to do more, and have never done anything more interesting, then this is even better. You would have discovered your dream job even before starting college.
